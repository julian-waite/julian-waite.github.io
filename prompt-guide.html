How to Write Better Prompts
A practical guide to getting the best from Claude and other AI tools
julian-waite.github.io
Prompt Engineer — Challenge Mode

Seven principles in order of impact. The first three will improve every prompt you write today. The last four separate good prompts from exceptional ones. Each principle includes a before/after example so you can see the difference in practice.

01 Be specific about the output, not just the task Foundation
The single biggest lever. Most prompts describe what to do but not what the result should look like. Specify format, length, tone, and structure — Claude will follow them precisely.
Before
Write me a summary of behavioural economics.
After
Write a 3-paragraph summary of behavioural economics for a non-academic audience. Plain English, no jargon. Cover: what it is, why it matters, one real-world example.
Rule of thumb: If you would brief a human writer with these details, brief Claude the same way.
02 Give Claude the context it needs to do a great job Foundation
Claude knows nothing about your situation unless you tell it. Who is this for? What's the purpose? What constraints are you working within? Context transforms generic answers into useful ones.
Before
Help me write an email about a delayed project.
After
Help me write to a senior stakeholder at a law firm about a 3-week CRM delay caused by data migration. Relationship is strong but professional. Transparent but confident — not defensive.
Ask yourself: What would someone need to know before they could give me genuinely useful advice on this?
03 Say who the audience is Foundation
The same information explained to a PhD researcher and a school student should look completely different. Audience shapes vocabulary, depth, examples and assumptions. Skip this and Claude guesses.
Before
Explain the COM-B model.
After
Explain COM-B to a senior HR director with no psychology background but strong commercial instincts. Use a workplace example. No academic language.
Useful signals: role, expertise level, familiarity with topic, what they care about, what they already know.
04 Ask for one thing at a time Advanced
Multi-part prompts produce unfocused answers. Claude tries to do everything at once and does each part less well. If you need multiple things, ask sequentially or structure the priority clearly.
Before
Help with my LinkedIn profile, write a cover letter, and give me salary negotiation advice.
After
Let's take these one at a time. First: review my LinkedIn headline and suggest three alternatives that position me as a behavioural science consultant rather than a sales leader.
Exception: Related things that belong together are fine — a brief, a structured plan, a sectioned document.
05 Tell Claude what role or perspective to take Advanced
Claude can reason from many positions — coach, critic, domain expert, devil's advocate. Specifying a role shapes not just the tone but the entire frame of reference used to construct the answer.
Before
What do you think of this business idea?
After
Act as a sceptical investor who has seen many behaviour change startups fail. Give me your three biggest concerns. Be direct — I need challenge, not validation.
Useful roles: expert in [field], devil's advocate, experienced editor, someone who disagrees, a first-time reader who knows nothing about this.
06 Use structure to organise complex prompts Advanced
For longer prompts, structure helps Claude process what you need. Separate context from task from constraints. Use labels. The more organised your prompt, the more organised the answer.
Before
I've got 20 years in sales and I study psychology and want to build a business and know how to position myself and what to offer...
After
Background: 20 years commercial leadership, studying applied psychology. Goal: Niche consulting at the intersection of sales and behavioural science. Task: Suggest 3 positioning angles, with a one-sentence description and target client for each.
Labels that help: Background / Goal / Task / Constraints / Format / Tone. Use only what's relevant.
07 Iterate — treat the first response as a draft Power
The best results rarely come from one prompt. Use the first response as a starting point, then refine: make it shorter, change the tone, go deeper on one section, try a different angle.
Before
[Reads response] OK that's fine I guess.
After
Good start. Now: shorten the first paragraph by half, make the tone less formal, and add a concrete example to point three. Keep everything else the same.
Useful follow-ups: "Make this more [concise/direct/conversational]" · "Expand on point 2" · "Rewrite for [audience]" · "What have you left out?"
Before you submit — quick checklist
Have I said what format I want?
Have I given the context needed?
Have I specified the audience?
Have I said what tone I want?
Is the task actually clear?
Have I said what length is right?
Am I asking one thing, or too many?
Would a specific example help?
