About

I worked for some great companies that did wonderful things. But the larger they got, the more work seemed to fall into frameworks that felt one size fits all — and the harder it became to do things that actually made a difference to people.

My strongest contributions were always the same: hiring really good people and creating an environment where they could perform. Sometimes that meant thinking outside the corporate boxes. What I noticed, again and again, was that people do their best work when they feel a genuine sense of autonomy, when they can master what they're doing and when they're surrounded by colleagues who are engaged and care about the same things. Those conditions are hard to create in a ten thousand person company. But within the microcosm of a well-led team, they're possible — and when you get it right, incredible things are possible.

My belief is a simple one: great strategy, great technology and great brands all matter. But none of it lands without the right people to bring it to life. It's the individual on the front line and the first line leader above them where it becomes real, where it gets tested and where it either works or it doesn't. Those are the people I've spent my career closest to — and they're the ones I want to focus on.

What I've seen, again and again, is that top performers — the people who want to do good work, who want to achieve their full potential and who want to feel like they're making a real difference — will do their best work and are more likely to stay when the right conditions are in place. Not just the right salary or the right title, but the right environment. One where they feel valued, connected and purposeful. Creating those conditions isn't the soft stuff. It's the thing that actually moves the needle — for the individual and for the business.

That's why I want to step out of corporate. That's why I want to take more control over my future. And that's where I think I can make a difference.

Behavioural psychology

We all know we should exercise more and eat better. We rarely do. That gap between knowing and doing is what fascinates me — and it shows up everywhere. In business it's the same question: how do you actually bring people with you in a lasting way, rather than relying on short term incentives or forcing change from the top? What makes people consistently work differently and genuinely believe in what they're doing?
